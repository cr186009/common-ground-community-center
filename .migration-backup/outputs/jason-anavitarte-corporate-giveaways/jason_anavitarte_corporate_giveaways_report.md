# Jason Anavitarte Corporate Giveaways Tracker

Prepared from Jason Anavitarte's Georgia Senate vote-history spreadsheets for the `2021-2022 regular`, `2021 special`, `2023-2024 regular`, `2023 special`, and `2025-2026 regular` sessions.

## Bottom line

This tracker found **17 bills** where Jason Anavitarte voted **Yea** on measures that created, extended, protected, clarified, or expanded tax credits, tax exemptions, grants, fee reductions, or similar financial advantages for corporations, industries, developers, or selected business sectors.

The strongest messaging targets are:

1. **HB 1291 (2022)**, which extended Georgia's high-technology and data-center sales-tax breaks and lowered some rural-county entry thresholds to as little as **5 jobs and $25 million** in investment.
2. **HB 408 (2023)**, which extended the sales-tax exemption on construction materials for hand-picked “competitive projects of regional significance” through **December 31, 2026**.
3. **HB 328 (2022 final action)**, which reduced annual right-of-way fees paid to municipalities by certain telecom infrastructure companies.
4. **HB 1053 (2022)**, which extended the postproduction-company tax credit inside Georgia's much larger film incentive system.
5. **HB 997 (2022)** and **SB 474 (2022)**, which created local property-tax carveouts for timber producers and agricultural aircraft.

## Important cautions

- This is **not** a claim that every listed bill was purely bad policy. Some are mixed or arguable.
- **HB 1041** (rural hospital tax credit cap increase) is best treated as a **public-interest or mixed** credit, not a classic corporate giveaway.
- I excluded broad individual tax rebates and broadly applicable personal tax cuts because they are not clean “corporate giveaway” fits.
- I also excluded votes that **curbed or repealed** business tax breaks, including later data-center rollback efforts, because they cut against the core thesis and would weaken the factual case if ignored.

## Top hits

### 1. HB 1291 (2022)
- **Category:** Data centers / high-technology exemptions
- **Why it matters:** This is the clearest corporate-giveaway vote in the set. The bill extended Georgia's data-center and high-tech equipment sales-tax exemptions and made rural-county qualification possible at very low permanent-job thresholds.
- **Best fact:** A 2025 DOAA summary estimated **$474.2 million** in forgone state revenue in **FY 2025** from the data-center exemption program alone, and also noted local tax abatements on representative projects.
- **Message angle:** Big tax breaks, few permanent jobs, and local taxpayers still exposed to infrastructure strain.

### 2. HB 408 (2023)
- **Category:** Other business-friendly revenue reductions
- **Why it matters:** Extended a construction-materials sales-tax exemption for selected “competitive projects of regional significance” from **June 30, 2023** to **December 31, 2026**.
- **Message angle:** If an ordinary homeowner buys materials, they pay tax. If a state-selected mega-project does it, Anavitarte voted to keep the carveout going.

### 3. HB 328 (2021-2022)
- **Category:** Local or special-interest tax breaks
- **Why it matters:** Reduced annual right-of-way use fees paid to municipalities by certain telecom companies.
- **Message angle:** Cities lose revenue, but fiber and telecom firms pay less. That's a classic example of shifting pressure onto local budgets.

### 4. HB 1053 (2022)
- **Category:** Film / entertainment tax credits
- **Why it matters:** Extended the postproduction-company credit and helped preserve Georgia's broader entertainment incentive pipeline.
- **Best context:** A DOAA-commissioned 2023 evaluation projected the broader film tax credit at **$1.188 billion** in state tax expenditure in **FY 2026** and emphasized that most of the film credit has **no annual aggregate cap**.
- **Message angle:** The state keeps writing huge checks through the tax code, while many jobs tied to the program are temporary or project-based.

### 5. HB 997 (2022) and SB 474 (2022)
- **Category:** Local or special-interest tax breaks
- **Why they matter:** Both narrowed local property-tax bases for favored industries, one for timber equipment/products and one for agricultural aircraft.
- **Message angle:** When politicians promise property-tax relief but keep carving out special exemptions for selected industries, everyone else is left carrying more of the load.

## Campaign messaging ideas for Alec Miller

Use these as starting language, not as literal scripts.

- **Data centers:** “Jason Anavitarte voted to extend Georgia's data-center tax break even when the deal could be triggered in smaller counties with only a handful of permanent jobs. State auditors later estimated the exemption cost roughly **$474 million in a single fiscal year**.”
- **Local taxpayers:** “He voted to lower what some telecom companies pay cities for public rights-of-way and backed special property-tax carveouts for timber inventory and agricultural aircraft. When carveouts grow, local taxpayers get squeezed.”
- **Mega-project carveouts:** “Anavitarte kept voting for targeted tax exemptions on construction materials for state-selected mega-projects instead of fighting for broad relief regular Georgians can actually see.”
- **Film incentives:** “He voted to keep extending and maintaining Georgia's entertainment tax-credit machine, even as auditors projected the broader program would cost the state more than **$1.1 billion a year**.”
- **Pattern argument:** “This wasn't one isolated vote. It was a pattern: data centers, film credits, freight rail, tourism subsidies, developer credits, and local tax carveouts for favored industries.”
- **Fairness frame:** “This is not about being anti-business. It's about whether tax policy should be written for everyone, or carved up for the best-connected industries and projects.”

## Recommended factual framing

The cleanest and safest public case is to lead with **HB 1291**, then stack **HB 408**, **HB 328**, and one or two of the local property-tax carveouts (**HB 997** / **SB 474**) behind it. Those are the least ambiguous votes for campaign use.

The weaker or more nuanced items are **HB 1041** (rural hospital credit cap increase) and **HB 482**-style technical cleanups. They can stay in a research file, but they are not your best front-line message.
